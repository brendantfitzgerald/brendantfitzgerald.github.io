Going Gray(scale)

February 17, 2020 / brendan / Comments Off on Going Gray(scale)

To my total lack of surprise, I’m not the first person to do this or write about it. Here’s a piece from Nellie Bowles in the New York Times, and another from Josh Hoffman on Medium. Both of these people set out with a goal in mind, but here’s my experience discovering this life hack.

For the past four months, I’ve had my cell phone set to grayscale. Recently, I’ve done the same to my work and home computers that run Windows. While I can’t tell you what originally inspired me to do this (a fascination with e-ink displays, a hatred of fun, a traumatic event involving printer toner, who knows), I can tell you what I’ve noticed since making the change.

Many people I know, myself included, have compulsions about using the web. Bored? Go to Reddit/Facebook/Instagram/Twitter. I use Reddit as a means of avoiding thinking, especially if the thoughts are unpleasant. This compulsion has been reduced since going grayscale. I credit this to two sides of the same coin:
1. The thoughtless, addictive media is less enjoyable without color, and
2. The type of media that lends itself to grayscale is typically more thoughtful and/or interesting to share. 

I’m not a zealot against color, but we are bombarded with it. All online content is manufactured, and color is another weapon in the arsenal of those who would sell you something. Red and yellow make people hungry, blue makes them calm, green is environmentally friendly, white is pure, black is death. Contrasting colors catch eyes. Eyes are money on the internet.

But maybe that rings hollow to you. You’ve got an adblocker, you can spot “sponsored content” a mile away, and you like colorful photos. Of course you do. We all do. That’s the issue. Have you ever explained a sunset to someone that wasn’t there for it? Perhaps a better question is, have you ever had a sunset explained to you? The sun’s setting as I type this, I’ll try:

The sun’s just gone down behind the trees, backlighting them. The sky’s a gradient of orange to amber, then a pale yellow, before the hard line of the purple clouds that are rolling slowly in from the East.

What did you get out of that? What message was I trying to convey? Writers far better than I have written vivid, iconic descriptions of scenes like the one outside my window. But unlike them, and more like a photo of a sunset, my description lacks any further meaning. I am not setting the scene to establish verisimilitude for the rest of my writing as in a novel, nor am I using my language in a poetic way to illustrate a more complex feeling. Just like a colorful photo without context, it means very little as an idea.

Now, when I’m browsing the web, my monkey brain never gets the chance to be awed by the transition of colors. And because these eye-catchers are no longer working, it’s easier to scroll past them on the way to the next piece of media that retains value in grayscale.

Text, obviously retains its full meaning in black and white. Sound too is unaffected by a change in color. Photos and videos that are well constructed and/or intended to be grayscale lose anywhere from very little to nothing. These formats also tend to be more concerned with communication of an idea as opposed to awe. Not that everything in black and white is worthwhile, the vast majority still isn’t. But without the awe of color, I find it much easier to filter.

Overall though, I find that I use the web less in total. When I do, I’m reading longer-form articles, and finding relevant information more quickly. An added side effect is that since most of my screen time is in grayscale, the colors of the real world are much more vibrant and meaningful. Almost like they don’t have to measure up to a high definition video of an octopus dreaming.
