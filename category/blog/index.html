Degoogling: Part 3 (Final?)

January 23, 2021 / brendan / Comments Off on Degoogling: Part 3 (Final?)

It’s now officially been a year since I switched from Gmail to Tutanota, and a handful of months since I left Drive for a Nextcloud instance. I’m still using Google Voice, Hangouts, Domains, Analytics, and Android.

I don’t know that I can say “Mission Accomplished”, given that I still use Google services, but I’m much more comfortable with my ability to remove Google from my digital life.

I intend to switch to an iPhone soon. I don’t like Apple’s stranglehold on the software, but their overtures towards individual privacy seem sincere.

Truthfully, I don’t use cloud storage and e-mail as much as I used to. In the past 2.5 years, I moved, changed jobs, got married, and a global pandemic happened.

I use Voice, Hangouts, and Domains frequently enough, and they’re good enough that I choose to continue to use them, even without the rest of the ecosystem.

Analytics started out as an experiment to see how much data was captured without much effort. It’s not a tremendous amount, but every time I go to delete it, I start digging through the data. Some day, I’ll cast it into the fire.

My last thought: Google doesn’t have strong network effects. In that way, it’s easy to leave. It sometimes feels like cutting off your nose to spite your face. Compare this to the recent mass push to move from WhatsApp (Facebook) to Signal (FOSS). I’ve read that in some parts of the world, it’s nearly impossible to live without WhatsApp. The cost to interact with anyone outside of that ecosystem is too high.

So, inspired by that and other recent events, I think I’ll document a few forays into the Fediverse, a collection of self-hosted, interconnected, compatible social media services.

Going Gray(scale)

February 17, 2020 / brendan / Comments Off on Going Gray(scale)

To my total lack of surprise, I’m not the first person to do this or write about it. Here’s a piece from Nellie Bowles in the New York Times, and another from Josh Hoffman on Medium. Both of these people set out with a goal in mind, but here’s my experience discovering this life hack.

For the past four months, I’ve had my cell phone set to grayscale. Recently, I’ve done the same to my work and home computers that run Windows. While I can’t tell you what originally inspired me to do this (a fascination with e-ink displays, a hatred of fun, a traumatic event involving printer toner, who knows), I can tell you what I’ve noticed since making the change.

Many people I know, myself included, have compulsions about using the web. Bored? Go to Reddit/Facebook/Instagram/Twitter. I use Reddit as a means of avoiding thinking, especially if the thoughts are unpleasant. This compulsion has been reduced since going grayscale. I credit this to two sides of the same coin:
1. The thoughtless, addictive media is less enjoyable without color, and
2. The type of media that lends itself to grayscale is typically more thoughtful and/or interesting to share. 

I’m not a zealot against color, but we are bombarded with it. All online content is manufactured, and color is another weapon in the arsenal of those who would sell you something. Red and yellow make people hungry, blue makes them calm, green is environmentally friendly, white is pure, black is death. Contrasting colors catch eyes. Eyes are money on the internet.

But maybe that rings hollow to you. You’ve got an adblocker, you can spot “sponsored content” a mile away, and you like colorful photos. Of course you do. We all do. That’s the issue. Have you ever explained a sunset to someone that wasn’t there for it? Perhaps a better question is, have you ever had a sunset explained to you? The sun’s setting as I type this, I’ll try:

The sun’s just gone down behind the trees, backlighting them. The sky’s a gradient of orange to amber, then a pale yellow, before the hard line of the purple clouds that are rolling slowly in from the East.

What did you get out of that? What message was I trying to convey? Writers far better than I have written vivid, iconic descriptions of scenes like the one outside my window. But unlike them, and more like a photo of a sunset, my description lacks any further meaning. I am not setting the scene to establish verisimilitude for the rest of my writing as in a novel, nor am I using my language in a poetic way to illustrate a more complex feeling. Just like a colorful photo without context, it means very little as an idea.

Now, when I’m browsing the web, my monkey brain never gets the chance to be awed by the transition of colors. And because these eye-catchers are no longer working, it’s easier to scroll past them on the way to the next piece of media that retains value in grayscale.

Text, obviously retains its full meaning in black and white. Sound too is unaffected by a change in color. Photos and videos that are well constructed and/or intended to be grayscale lose anywhere from very little to nothing. These formats also tend to be more concerned with communication of an idea as opposed to awe. Not that everything in black and white is worthwhile, the vast majority still isn’t. But without the awe of color, I find it much easier to filter.

Overall though, I find that I use the web less in total. When I do, I’m reading longer-form articles, and finding relevant information more quickly. An added side effect is that since most of my screen time is in grayscale, the colors of the real world are much more vibrant and meaningful. Almost like they don’t have to measure up to a high definition video of an octopus dreaming.

Degoogling, Less and Less Intimidating.

February 2, 2020 / brendan / Comments Off on Degoogling, Less and Less Intimidating.

What’s changed.

Migrated from Gmail to Tutanota.
Using addresses on my own domain is so empowering. Even if I want to move mail services again, as long as I keep the domain, I’ll never have to send out another “I changed my address” message.
I’ve replicated the “tab” functionality of Gmail’s inbox with alias addresses (e.g. myname.subscriptions.xx@yesfitz.com). It needs some tweaks to bring it more in-line with Gmail’s delineations. I imagine a lot of thinking went into them doing it the way they did, and who am I to question that?

Stopped using Google Authenticator.
I was using Google’s Authenticator for 2FA. Now I’m using Authy by Twilio. Because it’s based on a shared secret between the service you’re logging into and the app on your phone, it shouldn’t matter which app you’re using. But then why risk using Google’s version?

Started using DuckDuckGo.
This is a tough one. Since I stopped using AltaVista (c. 2003), I’ve searched with Google. That’s a very long time being initiated into the Google way of searching. That’s a pain point for DuckDuckGo. You don’t get the same results with the same query.

However, about five years ago, Google started getting a little too fresh with my queries. Questioning my use of quotation marks, ignoring words in the query, allowing (what it thought were) synonyms of words in my query to register as results. While I grew up learning to talk to the Google search bar, you can’t have a productive relationship with a tool that doesn’t respect you as the user.

With that fresh mindset, I’m feeling much more excited about DuckDuckGo. And if I need to go back to Google, I can just add “!g” to my query in DuckDuckGo, and it brings me to the Google results. And there’s about 13,504 more “bangs” you can use with DuckDuckGo.

Moved over to Firefox from Chrome.
It was installed on most of my devices. Now it’s installed and the default on all of them. I’m using the add-ons Privacy Badger from EFF, uBlock Origin by Raymond Hill, and Redirect AMP to HTML by Daniel Aleksandersen. Still need to get Firefox working at my office computer.

I’m writing this from Firefox on the Linux Mint partition on my Chromebook.
Sure, it doesn’t have audio firmware or hotkey support. And that’s a pretty big issue. Otherwise though, I think I’m ready to move over to Linux whole-hog.

What hasn’t changed. Yet.

Still using Google Domains as a registrar.
Looking at the prices, the horror stories, and my own appetite for degoogling, I’ll stick with Google for now. I suppose I could use Route 53 via Amazon Web Services. But that’s for another time.

Using Google Analytics.
Luckily, I’ve yet to have any visitors from the EU since using Analytics, so no one with standing can prove I’m violating GDPR. I remember someone showing off their privacy-protecting analytics platform on Hacker News a few weeks ago. I’ll be looking into that soon.

Using Hangouts/Google Voice.
I have a Google Voice number. It’s been incredibly helpful for international calling, as well as being able to text over data connections. Unlike e-mail, finding another SMS-enabled VoIP provider has not been easy or cheap. But I’m comfortable enough with it for now.

Using an Android Phone.
I’ll call it serendipity that I cracked the glass screen on the otherwise all-plastic phone since I wrote the initial degoogling post. While it’s still 95% usable, it gives me an excuse to look for a hack-friendly phone. I’ve read good things about the Xiaomi Mi A3. And it’s cheap enough that I won’t feel too bad if it became relegated to a car computer. God knows the screen is big enough.

In the meantime, I’ve installed F-Droid, a free/libre alternative to the Google Play Store. But it’s not very helpful. There’s a lot of flashlight apps, weather apps, and ugly, simple games.
I wish that everyone could agree that libre (free as in speech), free (free as in beer), and quality are the legs of a three-legged stool problem. I honestly enjoy paying for quality products and services. It makes me feel good to be able to materially support the producer, no matter if it’s an independent programmer, or a $10 billion multinational. This is a tired realization, but we do pay money for every private service we use online. If you don’t pay them with money, you’re the one being used.
Caveat: you can still pay money and be used.

Takeaways

So far, degoogling isn’t as hard as I feared.
Some would argue that I haven’t actually degoogled, given that I’m still willingly using Google services. But it’s mostly been a proof-of-concept on leaving a walled-garden. I knew that leaving was possible, but I wasn’t sure how difficult it would actually be in-practice. The Google services I still use are either on the chopping block, or I’ve looked at alternatives and decided that Google is better.

I’d recommend everyone get their own domain.
Even if you register it with Google, even if you have no interest in running a website, even if you just forward the e-mail address to your Gmail account. Having your own domain gives you the option to go anywhere. $12 a year is a small price to pay for the ability to walk away.

Degoogling, An Intimidating Proposition

December 22, 2019 / brendan / Comments Off on Degoogling, An Intimidating Proposition

Why I’m trying to degoogle.

HTTPS decryption/reencryption awoke something in me.
In early November, I discovered that it’s common for workplaces to decrypt all web traffic, inspect it, and reencrypt it before delivering it to the user. While fully acknowledging that using someone else’s network means you shouldn’t have any expectation of privacy, this explicit eavesdropping is different than a possible risk.
I’ll write more of my thoughts on this strategy for information security in a separate post, but it made me start thinking seriously about my privacy again.

Youtube users being banned and unable to access e-mail.
Also in early November, YouTube users were banned for spamming emojis in Markiplier’s stream, despite the fact that he requested them to. No big deal, right?
It wouldn’t have been, except that their entertainment-purpose YouTube account was also tied to their more essential services like Gmail, Google Calendar, Google Docs, etc. It would be like if Verizon could shut off your cell phone because you talked in a movie theater. The convenience of having one service provider creates a dangerous concentration of power.

Reading about AMP.
AMP is a Google-endorsed framework for creating webpages that load quickly on mobile. AMP pages will rank much higher in Google searches than their non-AMP counterparts. This is probably more of a complaint about the concentration of power in search, and Google has used their sway for good before, (ranking HTTPS pages higher), but AMP has more to do with control than security or efficiency. You need to play by Google’s rules, and in return, they’ll give you higher ranking, but not much else. Your content effectively belongs to Google, but they’ll keep your brand on it, as long as you do all the work. And if you don’t, your competition will.

Virtualizing my home server.
Finally, a positive reason for degoogling. I’ve been slowly building out the home server for Annie and I. What started out as a simple file server, now runs Plex, a home Wiki, and LibreNMS, all on one Ubuntu instance. Now I need to fix my mess. Once I started planning out how to virtualize the server, it inspired me to start self-hosting as much as I could.

How I’m succeeding.

Decided on an email provider.
Self-hosted: Absolutely no redundancy, and a lot of work for at most two people to use.
Fastmail: Australian company. Don’t want to support a country that
explicitly flouts data privacy. (Unironically written from America.)
Protonmail: Expensive for e-mail without Calendar or Contacts
management.
Tutanota: Just right. Affordable, with Calendar and Contacts.

Worst case scenario, I can always export my data and take my e-mail address to a different provider.

Started making a conscious effort to use Firefox and Linux.
My Mint partition on my Chromebook is all good to go. On Windows and OS X, I’m getting better about using Firefox instead of Chrome, although it can be difficult at work because there’s less emphasis to support Firefox internally.

Donated to Mozilla, Wikipedia, EFF, FreeSoftwareFoundation, some others.
December 3rd is Giving Tuesday. I try to donate to meaningful projects once a year. Less helpful than committing to a year of donations, but I like to consider it paying for their products that I used in the last year. That way, if they do something I disagree with in the new year, I can adjust my payment accordingly.

How I’m failing.

I’m writing this on my Chromebook.
Because I’m still on Google services, it’s a lot easier to open up the Chromebook into ChromeOS, check my mail, do my Duolingo, and hop back off.

I’m still on Gmail.
Things have been pretty busy at work, and I want to make sure that I have the time and energy to dedicate to degoogling before I go distributing my new e-mail address.

I’m very nervous about moving to a google-free Android.
Sure, I could buy an iPhone. But I like Android. I like the variety of hardware, and the customization of the interface. If I can reliably move to a LineageOS phone, I’ll gladly do it. I’m already running an older Sony phone, pre-fingerprint reader even, so I won’t miss any features added in the past four years, but I’d still like to be able to call, text, and e-mail.

Takeaways.

Degoogling is really hard, which in a way makes it worth it.
That may sound silly, but in a way, it feels like trying to kick an addiction. I don’t want to have to use Google services. So, after I recreate a similar environment without Google, I’ll be able to decide whether or not the convenience of their service is worth the price of my privacy.
